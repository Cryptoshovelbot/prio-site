Best Neighborhoods in Barcelona for Expats in 2026

March 202612 min read

Choosing the right neighborhood in Barcelona is just as important as finding the right apartment. Each barrio has its own personality, price range, and lifestyle. This guide gives you the honest version — not the tourist brochure version — of what it's actually like to live in each neighborhood as an expat in 2026.

We've helped dozens of apartment hunters in Barcelona find their place through Prio's real-time alerts, and we've seen which neighborhoods attract the most search activity, which have the fastest-moving listings, and where the best value lies.

How to Choose: The 3 Questions

Before diving into neighborhoods, answer these three questions. They'll narrow your search immediately:

  • Budget: Under €800/month = Poble Sec, Sants, Sant Andreu. €800-1,100 = most central neighborhoods. Over €1,100 = Eixample Dreta, El Born, Sarrià.
  • Lifestyle: Nightlife = Gràcia, Raval. Quiet = Sarrià, Poble Sec upper. Beach = Poblenou, Barceloneta. Culture = El Born, Gòtic.
  • Commute: If you work in 22@ = Poblenou. Central offices = Eixample. Remote = anywhere with good WiFi (everywhere in BCN).

The Neighborhoods, Ranked by Expat Popularity

1. Eixample — The Default Choice

Wide avenues, Modernista architecture, and the highest concentration of rental listings in Barcelona. Eixample is divided into Esquerra (left, more residential, slightly cheaper) and Dreta (right, closer to Passeig de Gràcia, pricier). Most first-time expats end up here because it's central, well-connected, and has the most apartments available.

Pros: Most listings = most options. Excellent metro access (L1, L2, L3, L4, L5 all cross Eixample). Wide streets with lots of natural light. Supermarkets on every block. Safe at all hours.

Cons: Can feel impersonal — it's a grid, not a village. Tourist-heavy on the Dreta side near Sagrada Família. Older buildings mean no elevator, poor insulation, and interior-facing apartments with limited light. Noisy on main streets (Gran Via, Aragó).

The honest take: Eixample is the safe choice, not the exciting one. You'll be comfortable but you might not fall in love with your neighborhood. That said, it's where the most listings are — so if speed matters (and it does in Barcelona), starting here maximizes your chances.

1-bed: €950–1,300/mo · 2-bed: €1,300–1,800/mo
Best for: first-time expats, professionals, couples who want convenience over charm.

2. Gràcia — The Village

Gràcia is the neighborhood that makes people fall in love with Barcelona. Narrow streets, plaças (small squares) where people gather in the evening, independent shops, and a genuine community feeling. It was an independent town until 1897, and it still feels like one.

Pros: Best community atmosphere in Barcelona. Excellent food — local restaurants, not tourist traps. Quieter than central BCN. The Gràcia festa in August is legendary. Strong French, German, and Italian expat communities.

Cons: Fewer listings because people rarely leave. Apartments are smaller. Some streets are very narrow (noise echoes). Parking is nearly impossible. Further from the beach than most central neighborhoods.

The honest take: If you get an apartment in Gràcia, you've won the Barcelona lottery. But be prepared to wait — listings move fast and there are fewer of them. Set up alerts specifically for Gràcia and be ready to call within minutes.

1-bed: €900–1,200/mo · 2-bed: €1,200–1,600/mo
Best for: creatives, foodies, people who value community over convenience.

3. Poble Sec — Best Value in Central Barcelona

Tucked between Montjuïc mountain and Avinguda Paral·lel, Poble Sec offers something rare in Barcelona: central location at non-central prices. The neighborhood has gentrified significantly but still maintains character. Carrer Blai's pintxos bars are a local institution.

Pros: 20-30% cheaper than Eixample for similar quality. Walking distance to Raval and the port. Montjuïc park access. Growing expat community. Good mix of local and international. Great food on Blai and Poeta Cabanyes.

Cons: Hills (upper Poble Sec is steep). Fewer metro options (L2, L3 at Paral·lel). Some streets feel isolated at night in upper areas. Less retail than Eixample.

1-bed: €800–1,100/mo · 2-bed: €1,100–1,400/mo
Best for: budget-conscious expats who still want a central location.

4. Sant Antoni — The Trendy One

Sant Antoni has undergone the most dramatic transformation of any Barcelona neighborhood in the last decade. The renovated Mercat de Sant Antoni anchors the area, surrounded by specialty coffee shops, brunch spots, and concept stores. Sunday morning's book market on Ronda Sant Antoni is a Barcelona tradition.

Pros: Best brunch scene in Barcelona (hands down). Excellent location between Eixample and Raval. Metro L2 (Sant Antoni station). Young, dynamic atmosphere. The market is spectacular.

Cons: Rents have risen 25%+ in 3 years as the area became fashionable. Some streets still have a rough Raval-adjacent edge. Gentrification tension is real — not everyone is happy about the changes.

1-bed: €950–1,250/mo · 2-bed: €1,300–1,700/mo
Best for: millennials, foodies, people who want trendy without Eixample prices.

5. Poblenou — Beach + Tech

Barcelona's 22@ innovation district has transformed this former industrial neighborhood into a tech hub. Newer buildings, coworking spaces, and direct beach access make it attractive for remote workers and tech professionals. Rambla del Poblenou retains a local village atmosphere.

Pros: Beach walkable. Newer buildings (elevator, AC, insulation). Coworking spaces everywhere. Growing restaurant scene. Less touristy than Barceloneta.

Cons: Feels disconnected from "real" Barcelona. Less nightlife than central areas. Can feel empty on weekday evenings. The 22@ area has soulless corporate architecture in parts.

1-bed: €950–1,300/mo · 2-bed: €1,300–1,700/mo
Best for: tech workers, digital nomads, beach lovers, anyone who prefers modern comfort over historic charm.

6. El Born / Ciutat Vella — The Beautiful Risk

El Born is objectively one of the most beautiful neighborhoods in Barcelona. Medieval streets, the Basilica de Santa Maria del Mar, Parc de la Ciutadella, and some of the city's best cocktail bars. But there's a catch.

Pros: Stunning architecture. Best cocktail bars. Walking distance to the beach and Barceloneta. Rich cultural life with galleries and museums.

Cons: Extremely touristy. Noisy until 3-4am on weekends. Higher scam risk for rentals (tourist apartment conversions). Older buildings with maintenance issues. Expensive for what you get in terms of apartment quality.

1-bed: €1,000–1,400/mo · 2-bed: €1,400–1,900/mo
Best for: people who prioritize beauty and nightlife, and can handle tourist crowds.

7. Sarrià–Sant Gervasi — The Quiet Life

Upper Barcelona. Leafy streets, family-friendly atmosphere, international schools, and a pace of life that feels nothing like the chaos of the city center. This is where Barcelona's old money lives, alongside a significant international community.

Pros: Quietest central-adjacent area. Excellent international schools. Safe, clean, well-maintained. Larger apartments than central BCN. FGC train gives decent connectivity.

Cons: Expensive. Far from the beach and nightlife. Can feel suburban. Less international restaurant diversity. You'll need the FGC — metro doesn't cover most of Sarrià.

1-bed: €1,100–1,500/mo · 2-bed: €1,500–2,200/mo
Best for: families, professionals with budget, anyone who values peace over nightlife.

Neighborhoods to Consider on a Budget

If your budget is under €800/month for a 1-bedroom, look at: Sants (excellent transport hub, local feel, €700-900), Sant Andreu (far from center but genuine Barcelona, €600-800), Horta-Guinardó (hilly, green, affordable, €650-850), and Les Corts (near Camp Nou, residential, €750-950).
